Minute 0 — The Ask Before the Theory

opening ask

Thanks for taking the time to hear this.

I want to be very clear about the shape of the ask before I describe the idea.

I am not asking anyone to validate a finished theory today. I am not asking PorterShed to adopt this. I am not even asking for a decision on whether I am right.

I am asking for help with the safest next step.

I believe I may have found a recoverable structure inside completed sentences. If that holds, it could become a sentence-audit layer for AI output, translation, policy language, legal text, agent instructions, and any domain where language must be trusted before it governs action.

So the ask is this: help me protect it, test it, and commercialise it without exposing it too widely too early.

The right next step is not publicity. It is controlled validation.

That means IP advice, trusted technical review, translation testing, AI product thinking, and commercial guidance.

I am bringing it to PorterShed because I need the right route into those conversations.

Minute 2 — The Metaphor

slide card 1

The best way I can explain the project is this: meaning is the train. Structure is the track. Humans ride the train. LLMs predict the train. But almost nobody looks at the track.

When we hear a sentence, meaning arrives so quickly that we do not see the structure that made meaning possible. We ride the train to the destination and forget that there was a track beneath us.

Settlement Gate is an attempt to hold the sentence open long enough to see the track.

Minute 3 — The First Observation

slide card 1

A sentence is not complete merely because the speaker stops speaking. If I say, “The man who…” and stop, the words have stopped, but the sentence has not completed. Something in you waits.

Completion is the point where the sentence has satisfied its own demand. A sentence is not complete because it stops. It stops properly because it has become complete.

Minute 4 — The Broken Sentence

slide card 2

Did you put the dog?

Most people immediately feel that something is wrong. It is not an intellectual response first. It is a bodily response: a little jolt, a small recoil, a waiting.

The sentence has created a demand it has not satisfied. You know it is broken before you explain why.

Back to top

Minute 5 — Why the Broken Sentence Matters

slide card 3

The broken sentence matters because it exposes what the working sentence hides. When a normal sentence completes, we receive meaning and move on. When a sentence fails, structure becomes visible because the mind searches for the missing landing.

That is the first glimpse of the track.

Back to top

Minute 6 — The Other Kind of Break

slide card 4

Did you put the dog out, milk?

This fails differently. “Did you put the dog out?” had already landed. Then “milk” arrived after closure and damaged the completed act.

So completion has edges. There is before-completion failure, and there is after-completion failure.

slide card 5

Minute 11 — The Sentence as Inheritable Unit

slide card 6

The sentence is the smallest unit of language that can be inherited. Legal clauses, policy rules, medical instructions, translations, and AI-agent commands travel as completed sentences.

Once a sentence lands, another person or system may act from it.

Minute 12 — Why This Matters for AI

slide card 7
