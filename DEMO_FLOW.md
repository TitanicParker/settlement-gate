# Demo Flow

<p><strong>Purpose:</strong> show that the machinery exists without trying to show every sentence.</p>

<details open>
<summary><strong>Core demo principle</strong></summary>

Use a small number of examples to make the phenomenon obvious, then show that the repository contains a repeatable inspection and resolver workflow behind those examples.

</details>

---

## Room setup

**Presenter:** originator of the concept.

**Listener:** Anthony at PorterShed.

**Target duration:** 30 minutes.

**Primary outcome:** secure guidance on the safest next step: protection, trusted validation, and commercial route.

---

## Demo arc

```text
1. Completion is felt
2. Broken sentences expose completion
3. Similar sentences can land differently
4. The A-packet slows the sentence down
5. The resolver gives an address
6. A receipt records the commitment
7. Translation becomes a pressure test
8. AI auditability becomes the commercial possibility
9. Ask Anthony for the safest next step
```

---

## Example 1 — Failure to close

**Sentence:**

> “Did you put the dog?”

**Point:**

The listener knows the sentence has not landed before they can explain the grammar.

**Presenter move:**

Pause after saying it. Let the incompletion be felt.

---

## Example 2 — Going past closure

**Sentence:**

> “Did you put the dog out, milk?”

**Point:**

The sentence had already closed at “out.” The extra word violates the landing.

**Presenter move:**

Use this as the contrast: one sentence fails before closure; the other goes past closure.

---

## Example 3 — Similar surface, different landing

**Sentences:**

> “The door closed.”

> “The door is closed.”

**Point:**

One leaves an event. The other leaves a state.

**Presenter move:**

Do not over-explain. The difference is obvious, and that obviousness is the point.

---

## Example 4 — A-packet inspection

**Sentence:**

> “The transport plan is now approved by Galway City Council.”

**Point:**

The A-packet slows the sentence down so the structural commitment becomes visible.

**What to show:**

```text
A1 — What survives?
A2 — What is governed?
A3 — What work is done?
A4 — Where does it hold?
A5 — What shape remains?
A6 — What rival reading must not win?
```

**Presenter move:**

Describe A1–A6 as an instrument, not as the whole theory.

---

## Example 5 — Conditional permission

**Sentence:**

> “Visitors may board the next ferry after ticket inspection.”

**Point:**

The sentence does not grant unconditional permission. Ticket inspection governs boarding.

**Presenter move:**

Connect this to AI agents: an agent must not act on “may board” while ignoring “after ticket inspection.”

---

## Example 6 — English–Irish pressure test

**Pattern:**

English and Irish sentence pairs in the Galway packet resolve to the same structural landings.

**Point:**

This does not prove universality. It shows a testable route: faithful translation should preserve address.

**Presenter move:**

Say “pressure test,” not “proof.”

---

## Final move — The ask

Return to Anthony and PorterShed.

**Ask:**

Help me take the next step safely: protect the idea, identify the right first reviewers, test the translation and AI-audit claims, and explore commercial development without losing discovery status or value.

---

## What not to do in the demo

- Do not show every sentence.
- Do not explain all 54 cells.
- Do not present the system as proven.
- Do not let the talk become a technical tutorial.
- Do not make the listener work harder than necessary.

---

## What the demo must leave behind

Anthony should leave with four ideas:

1. Completed sentences settle something.
2. Some settlements should be inspected before governing action.
3. Settlement Gate has an instrument for recovering the landing.
4. The safe next step is protection plus trusted validation.
