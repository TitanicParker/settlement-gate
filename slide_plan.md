# Presentation Structure

## Slide 0 — The Ask to Anthony

Purpose: Ask for help protecting, testing, and commercialising the idea safely.

### Bullets
- Keep the idea protected before wide exposure.
- Find the safest first reviewers.
- Separate validation from commercialisation.
- Ask for guidance, not immediate adoption.

### Suggested visual

Simple title card: Anthony / PorterShed / protect-test-build.

### Speaker purpose

Open with the practical ask and set the confidential tone.

## Slide 1 — Completion Comes Before Stopping

Soundbite: A sentence is not complete because it stops. It stops because it has become complete.

### Bullets
- Completion is felt before it is explained.
- Stopping is not the same as closure.
- The sentence becomes inheritable only when complete.
- This is the doorway into the project.

### Suggested visual

One sentence stopping cleanly at a landing point.

### Speaker purpose

Frame sentence completion as the central phenomenon.

## Slide 2 — Broken Before Explained

Soundbite: You know a sentence is broken before you know why it is broken.

### Bullets
- People detect broken sentences before naming the rule.
- The reaction is ordinary, not academic.
- That recognition suggests hidden machinery.
- The project begins with that shared intuition.

### Suggested visual

Broken line ending before a visible landing point.

### Speaker purpose

Make the audience trust their own recognition before theory appears.

## Slide 3 — Failure to Close

Soundbite: “Did you put the dog?” exposes the structure by failing to close.

### Bullets
- The example stops too early.
- The listener waits for a missing completion.
- The structure is exposed by absence.
- This is failure to close.

### Suggested visual

The text “Did you put the dog?” with an open gap.

### Speaker purpose

Show incomplete closure with a memorable example.

## Slide 4 — Going Past Closure

Soundbite: “Did you put the dog out, milk?” exposes the structure by going past closure.

### Bullets
- The sentence had already landed.
- The extra word violates the completed form.
- The structure is exposed by excess.
- This is going past closure.

### Suggested visual

The text “Did you put the dog out, milk?” with an overrun marker.

### Speaker purpose

Show over-completion with the second dog example.

## Slide 5 — Meaning Hides the Machinery

Soundbite: Meaning is so successful that it hides the machinery that produced it.

### Bullets
- Meaning arrives quickly and hides the process.
- We usually see the result, not the machinery.
- Settlement Gate tries to inspect the machinery.
- The aim is recoverability, not abstraction.

### Suggested visual

A bright light switch with hidden wiring.

### Speaker purpose

Explain why the phenomenon is usually invisible.

## Slide 6 — The Smallest Inheritable Whole

Soundbite: The sentence is the smallest unit of language that can be inherited.

### Bullets
- A sentence can travel between people.
- It can be inherited, challenged, obeyed, or acted on.
- Laws, contracts, claims, and AI outputs arrive this way.
- Completion makes inheritance possible.

### Suggested visual

A single sentence travelling between two people.

### Speaker purpose

Connect sentence completion to inheritance and action.

## Slide 7 — AI Meets Us in Sentences

Soundbite: AI does not meet us in tokens. It meets us in completed sentences.

### Bullets
- AI may generate tokens internally.
- Humans inherit completed sentences.
- Action, trust, and accountability begin at sentence level.
- Important AI sentences need auditability.

### Suggested visual

Tokens in the background; completed sentence in the foreground.

### Speaker purpose

Connect the project to AI without overclaiming.

## Slide 8 — Every Sentence Lands Somewhere

Soundbite: Every completed sentence lands somewhere. That landing is its address.

### Bullets
- Address means structural landing, not topic.
- The landing says what the sentence has made stand.
- Different landings can have similar wording.
- The address may be recoverable.

### Suggested visual

A sentence landing on a map pin labelled address.

### Speaker purpose

Introduce address as the core working concept.

## Slide 9 — Similar Words, Different Landings

Soundbite: Two similar sentences can have different addresses: “The door closed” and “The door is closed.”

### Bullets
- “The door closed” leaves an event.
- “The door is closed” leaves a state.
- Similar words can settle different things.
- This motivates sentence-level receipts.

### Suggested visual

Two door sentences side by side with different labels: event / state.

### Speaker purpose

Show why address is different from surface similarity.

## Slide 10 — Slowing the Sentence Down

Soundbite: The A-packet slows the sentence down long enough for structure to become visible.

### Bullets
- The A-packet slows interpretation down.
- It asks what survives, governs, works, holds, remains, and must be excluded.
- It prevents easy collapse into topic or paraphrase.
- It makes the sentence inspectable.

### Suggested visual

A sentence entering six inspection gates labelled A1-A6.

### Speaker purpose

Introduce A1-A6 as the practical inspection method.

## Slide 11 — Instrument, Not Theory

Soundbite: A1 to A6 is not the theory. It is the instrument that lets us see the theory.

### Bullets
- A1-A6 is an instrument.
- It is not the whole theory.
- It prepares the resolver to assign an address.
- It supports reproducible pressure testing.

### Suggested visual

Instrument panel, microscope, or lens over a sentence.

### Speaker purpose

Prevent the listener from thinking A1-A6 is the whole theory.

## Slide 12 — Sentence Receipts

Soundbite: A receipt is what remains when a sentence has shown its commitments.

### Bullets
- A receipt is not a summary.
- It records commitments and blocked rival readings.
- It can help decide whether action is allowed.
- It turns completion into audit material.

### Suggested visual

A compact receipt attached to a sentence.

### Speaker purpose

Introduce receipts as the bridge from theory to audit.

## Slide 13 — Translation as Pressure Test

Soundbite: A successful translation should preserve the address, even when every word changes.

### Bullets
- Faithful translation should preserve settlement.
- Words and grammar may change.
- The landing should remain if the sentence function is equivalent.
- This makes translation testable.

### Suggested visual

Two languages, one shared landing marker.

### Speaker purpose

Frame translation as a testable pressure test.

## Slide 14 — Galway / Irish Demo

Soundbite: In the Galway packet, English and Irish sentence pairs resolve to the same structural landings.

### Bullets
- The packet includes five English-Irish pairs.
- The pairs resolve to shared landings locally.
- This is a pressure test, not universal proof.
- It shows a candidate validation pathway.

### Suggested visual

Five English-Irish pairings flowing into shared address cards.

### Speaker purpose

Use Galway / Irish material as local evidence only.

## Slide 15 — The Future Audit Label

Soundbite: The future I am imagining is simple: every important sentence carries a small audit label.

### Bullets
- Important sentences could carry small audit labels.
- Useful for AI, translation, legal, policy, and agent instructions.
- Labels support judgment rather than replacing it.
- The commercial form remains open.

### Suggested visual

Small audit label beside legal, AI, policy, and translation sentences.

### Speaker purpose

Describe the possible future product layer.

## Slide 16 — The Ask to Anthony and PorterShed

Purpose: Return to the safe next step: protect, validate, build.

### Bullets
- Do not overexpose before protection.
- Validate with trusted reviewers.
- Build the smallest useful demonstration.
- Use PorterShed as a safe first route.

### Suggested visual

Three-step path: protect, validate, build.

### Speaker purpose

Close with the safe next step.
